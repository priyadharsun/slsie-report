Section 01

Platform Overview & Architecture

SLSIE (Sri Lanka Sports Intelligence Ecosystem) is a web-based sports management and intelligence platform purpose-built for the National Olympic Committee of Sri Lanka (NOCSL). It consolidates federation governance, athlete health monitoring, gazette compliance enforcement, AI-powered analytics, and reporting into a single unified system accessible by authorised users from any device.

🏛️
34 National Sports Federations
All NSFs affiliated to NOCSL managed in one platform — governance scores, compliance status, EC officer registers, and athlete data.
🤖
Anthropic Claude AI Integration
Three AI capabilities — natural language chatbot, athlete coaching insights, and federation governance analysis — powered by Claude Haiku API.
⚖️
Gazette No. 2437/24 Compliance
National Sports Associations Regulations No. 01 of 2025 enforced automatically — grading, term limits, gender quotas, and 5-point compliance checklist.
📄
6 AI-Enhanced Reports
Governance, compliance, athlete risk, performance, Fit for Life, and donor impact reports — generated in seconds with AI executive summaries.

Governance Score Validation Workflow — Document Upload, Rule Engine & Human Approval

Federation officers cannot manually enter their own governance scores. Instead, SLSIE uses a three-stage validated workflow: federations upload supporting documents via a mobile app, an AI-powered rule engine analyses the documents and recommends dimension scores, and a designated NOCSL committee member acts as human-in-the-loop to review and approve the final scores. This eliminates self-reported bias and ensures every score is evidence-backed.

Why this matters: In many sports governance systems, federations self-report their scores — creating incentives to inflate ratings. SLSIE's document-driven workflow means a governance score of 4.5 in Financial Transparency can only be awarded if the federation has actually uploaded verified audited accounts that the rule engine confirms meet gazette standards. The human approver adds a final layer of accountability before any score is published on the benchmark.
1
📱 Mobile App — Document Upload
Federation officer logs into the SLSIE mobile app and uploads required evidence documents for each governance dimension — AGM minutes, audited financials, strategic plan, safeguarding policy, etc. Documents are securely transmitted to the SLSIE server for processing.
→
2
🤖 Rule Engine — AI Document Analysis
The SLSIE rule engine extracts key data points from each uploaded document — dates, officer names, financial figures, policy clauses — and cross-checks them against gazette requirements. The AI then recommends a score (1.0–5.0) per dimension with confidence level and evidence citations.
→
3
👤 Human-in-Loop — NOCSL Approval
A designated NOCSL Olympic Committee member receives an approval task. They review the uploaded documents alongside the AI-recommended scores, can override individual dimension scores with a written reason, and either approve or request revision. Only approved scores are published to the benchmark.

✅ Business Value of the Validation Workflow

  • Eliminates self-reported score inflation — federations cannot award themselves high scores; every score requires documentary evidence that the rule engine validates
  • Consistent, auditable scoring — the rule engine applies the same criteria to every federation, removing subjectivity and bias from the assessment process
  • Human accountability — the committee member approval step ensures a qualified person is responsible for every published score, with their identity and override reasoning logged permanently
  • Faster turnaround — AI pre-analysis reduces committee review time from hours to minutes per federation; human reviewers only need to verify, not read and score from scratch
  • Complete evidence trail — every published score is backed by stored documents, AI analysis output, and reviewer decision — defensible in any governance audit or legal challenge
  • Mobile-first for federations — removing the need for federations to log into a desktop system dramatically increases submission compliance, particularly for smaller federations with limited IT capacity

Section 08

Fit for Life Programme

The Fit for Life module is NOCSL's digital management system for the IOC Fit for Life 2025–2035 community sports development programme. Unlike the elite athlete modules, this programme serves non-elite community participants — ordinary citizens across all nine provinces of Sri Lanka who participate in sport for health, wellbeing, and social development.

About IOC Fit for Life 2025–2035: The International Olympic Committee launched Fit for Life as its flagship community sports strategy under Olympic Agenda 2020+5. The programme's goal is to use sport as a tool for reducing non-communicable diseases, improving mental health outcomes, and building social cohesion in underserved communities. NOCSL, as the IOC-affiliated national committee, is the primary implementing body for Sri Lanka and is required to report programme outcomes to the IOC annually. SLSIE enables NOCSL to collect, manage, and report this data digitally for the first time.

Donor & IOC Reporting

SLSIE generates a dedicated Donor Impact Report and a Fit for Life Programme Report directly from the data collected in this module. These reports replace weeks of manual data compilation that was previously required before each IOC review or donor progress meeting.

📄 IOC Annual Report Requirements
  • Total number of community participants by province, gender, and sport
  • Average health indicator improvements (BMI, wellbeing, sleep) from baseline to year-end
  • Total activity hours and session count — evidence of programme reach
  • Geographic coverage — provinces served and underserved region analysis
  • Trend data — year-on-year programme growth comparison
💰 UNDP / Donor Reporting Requirements
  • Number of direct beneficiaries reached — by demographic breakdown
  • Measurable health outcomes — percentage showing BMI improvement
  • Mental health impact — percentage reporting improved wellbeing scores
  • Budget utilisation evidence — cost per participant per outcome
  • SDG alignment evidence — linking programme outcomes to SDG 3 (Health) and SDG 10 (Inequality)

✅ Business Value

  • Demonstrates measurable programme reach and health impact to IOC, UNDP, and international donors with verified, real-time data
  • Provincial breakdown immediately shows geographic equity gaps — which regions are underserved and need additional investment
  • Monthly health indicator tracking shows whether the programme is delivering its core health objectives — not just participation numbers
  • Health outcome tracking supports NOCSL's mandatory reporting obligations under the IOC Fit for Life 2025–2035 framework
  • Donor Impact Report and Fit for Life Report are generated instantly from live data — eliminating 2–3 weeks of manual data compilation before each donor review
  • Digital evidence trail enables NOCSL to defend programme effectiveness to Ministry and justify continued funding requests

✅ Business Value — Mobile Health Sync

  • Eliminates the single biggest barrier to Fit for Life data quality — manual entry burden on coaches and participants which leads to missing, delayed, or inaccurate records
  • Continuous 24/7 health monitoring replaces weekly snapshot check-ins — enabling earlier detection of overtraining, poor recovery, and wellbeing decline at the programme level
  • Wearable data (VO₂ max, HRV, recovery scores) feeds directly into ACWR injury risk calculations — combining training load data with real-time recovery status for more precise risk alerts
  • Participants in remote provinces without dedicated coaches can still contribute health data automatically — closing the geographic equity gap in Fit for Life data collection
  • IOC Fit for Life 2025–2035 reporting requires verified, consistent health outcome data across all provinces — auto-sync from consumer devices provides audit-ready, timestamped evidence at no additional cost
  • The hybrid model (auto-sync + manual entry) is inclusive by design — participants without smartphones or wearables can still enter data manually, while those with devices get zero-effort tracking
